Don's conjecture for standardized DFAs

In an n-state completely reachable automaton, can every k-element subset of states be reached by a word of length ≤ n(n−k)? For standardized DFAs this is open; Zhu proved only the weaker n(n−k)+n−1.

Status: open conjecture. This page reports exact enumeration, an independent reaching-word verifier, and a bounded counterexample search — not a proof.

Setup

  • Image action. A word w acts on the whole state set: Q·w = {δ(q,w) : q ∈ Q}. A subset S has a reaching word w if Q·w = S.
  • Completely reachable (CRA). Every non-empty subset has a reaching word.
  • Standardized (Casas–Volkov normal form). b is the cycle q ↦ q+1 (mod n); a is a deficiency-1 map with excl(a) = {0} and dupl(a) = {a(0)} (exactly one merge); and the automaton is CRA.
  • Don's conjecture (open): reaching word of length ≤ n(n−k). Zhu (proved): ≤ n(n−k)+n−1.

What was built

  • Exact enumerator of all standardized CRA DFAs (the normal form, filtered to CRA by an exact subset-lattice BFS).
  • Shortest-reaching-word BFS over the 2ⁿ subsets (bitmask-encoded).
  • Independent verifier (separate module) that re-derives the image action and checks an explicit reaching word reaches its subset — the sole arbiter.
  • Bound checks for Don's n(n−k) and Zhu's n(n−k)+n−1.

Don's conjecture holds for every standardized completely reachable DFA with n ≤ 8, and the bound n(n−k) is tight — achieved with equality but never exceeded. Concrete certificate (n=5, a=(1,1,2,3,4), b=(1,2,3,4,0)): reaching the singleton {0} needs the shortest word abbbbabbbbabbbbabbbb of length 20 = 5·(5−1) = n(n−k) — independently verified.

Full run: don_dfa_results.json. 16 tests cross-check the BFS lengths against explicit verified reaching words.

Where this leaves the problem

The open conjecture is confirmed on the full standardized family up to n=8, with the extremal (bound-achieving) instances exhibited and certified. The natural next step is pushing n further with a smarter enumerator and mining the extremal DFAs for a structural pattern — short of the (open) conjecture itself.
